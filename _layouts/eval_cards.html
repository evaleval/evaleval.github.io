The Problem

Why Evaluation Cards?

Results are reported everywhere and in every way — but rarely with enough information to interpret them. Evaluation Cards fixes the reporting layer.

Fragmented Reporting

Scores live in leaderboards, model cards, blog posts, papers, and posters. With no shared standard, there is no way to compare results across sources or identify what a report leaves out.

Hidden Discrepancies

Three organizations report GPT-5's MATH-500 score anywhere from 84.7% to 98.9% — depending on configuration that is rarely fully reported. Evaluation Cards surfaces the discrepancies and why they exist.

From Scores to Claims

Evals are more than numbers — they are interpretive claims. By unflattening the model–benchmark–score triple, we recover the structure needed to answer deeper questions about what results actually mean.

Anatomy of a Card

Four interpretive signals, one record.

Across 100,000+ records, every card computes four signals — grounded in our user interviews with evaluation stakeholders. Explore them beside a live view of the underlying data.

01 Reproducibility
Can someone else run this evaluation and get the same results? We check for the minimal set of fields — like temperature and max-tokens — needed to re-run it. 96.5% of records are missing at least one.
02 Completeness
Is benchmark documentation sufficient to interpret scores? We assess construct definitions, validation, intended use, limitations, and provenance — median completeness today is just 10.7%.
03 Provenance
Who reported the score, and who has reproduced it? We distinguish first-party from third-party reporters and track corroboration across sources — 98.2% of pairs are reported by only one party.
04 Comparability
What is the measurement target, and how faithfully do different setups evaluate against it? We flag divergences that reflect configuration rather than capability — among multiply-reported pairs, scores diverge 51.9% of the time.

How it composes

One Canonical Record

Evaluation Cards composes the raw data the ecosystem already produces — at scale — into a single, interpretable evaluation record.

Run Data

Who ran the eval, on what model, with what generation settings — sourced from Every Eval Ever (EEE), the Coalition's standardized schema and community repository for eval run data.

Benchmark Metadata

The benchmark's measurement target, scoring, provenance, and known risks — drawn from IBM AutoBenchmarkCards, which extract documentation from papers and system cards.

Model Metadata

Release date, parameter count, and weight accessibility — pulled automatically from community catalogs (hub-stats, models.dev) covering both open and API-hosted models.

First results

A first glimpse of the whole ecosystem

Aggregating the four signals across the reporting ecosystem reveals startling early findings (5 June 2026 snapshot).

96.5%

Not reproducible

Of 50,000+ records, 96.5% are missing at least one field needed to re-run them. Max-tokens is absent 95.6% of the time, temperature 93.9%. Even 95% of GPT-5's documented results lack the parameters to reproduce them.

10.7%

Median completeness

Across all 635 benchmarks, median documentation completeness is a paltry 10.7%. Scores are present, but construct definitions, validation, intended use, and limitations are missing far more often than not.

51.9%

Scores diverge

98.2% of model–benchmark pairs are reported by only one party. Of the rest, scores from different parties diverge substantially more than half the time (51.9%).
